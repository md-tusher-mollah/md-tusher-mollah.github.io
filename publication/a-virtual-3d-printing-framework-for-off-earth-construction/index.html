A Virtual 3D Printing Framework for Off-Earth Construction

M.T. Mollah, B. Šeta, J. Spangenberg

Acta Astronautica (2026)

DOI Paper URL PDF Cite

Abstract

3D printing, due to its digital nature, offers a practical approach to autonomous off-Earth construction by utilizing local materials, thereby reducing the reliance on Earth-based resources and manpower. Implementing large-scale and rapid 3D printing of structures in extraterrestrial environments poses unique challenges due to conditions such as altered gravity, vacuum, and extreme thermal variations. These challenges are compounded by the fact that replicating altered gravity on Earth is not feasible for extended periods of time. Therefore, a virtual system is needed to advance the 3D printing capabilities. This paper presents computational fluid dynamics (CFD) model, validated through experimental 3D printing of lunar regolith simulant-based geopolymer on Earth. The model is used to simulate the 3D printing of structures under altered gravity conditions. The results provide insights into geometrical precision and deformation as gravity varies across different celestial bodies, as well as the influence of regolith mortar properties and nozzle diameter when focusing on printing on the Moon. The results highlight ways to control the curing kinetics of the lunar regolith mortar in order to print fast and on larger scale. Understanding construction under different gravity levels represents a transformative step towards realizing human dreams of colonizing and exploring other planets. The first step is establishing a self-sustaining lunar outpost, which has long been a key objective in advancing human space exploration [1,2]. Building infrastructure like bases, landing pads, and habitats requires various protective measures, including radiation protection, thermal insulation, exhaust plume containment, and micro-meteoroid shielding. Therefore, innovative solutions are essential from both material science and engineering perspectives. 3D printing technology was conceptualized for the construction of lunar habitats back in 2005 [3,4]. On Earth, 3D printing has demonstrated considerable potential in construction due to its unparalleled versatility and efficiency in fabricating complex structures. Given its digital nature, this technology is highly promising for offEarth construction, where minimal supervision will be required. There are, however, a variety of challenges arising from both the material and printing processes, as well as unknown structural behavior under altered environmental conditions. Transporting materials from Earth is impractical, underscoring the imperative of in-situ resource utilization (ISRU) [5,6]. The regolith available on the celestial bodies is the obvious choice for off-Earth construction. For instance, lunar regolith serves as a raw material for producing aggregates [7], binders [8,9], and reinforcements [3,10], ensuring its versatility. For extrusion-based 3D printing, regolith can be processed into feedstock composition in the form of filament, paste, or mortar using methods such as polymer-based regolith composites [14], sulfur concrete [6], and geopolymers [9]. These methods involve several stages, including crushing and grinding, binder selection and mixing, and rheology modification with additives and thixotropic agents [15, 16] before the actual printing process. Rheological characterization of the feedstock is essential for ensuring extrudability and identifying issues, such as interlayer bonding, that could affect print quality.
